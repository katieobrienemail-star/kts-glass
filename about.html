Artist Statement

I'm aiming for my work to sit at a meeting point of art and science. With a background in Fine Art (BA(Hons) UAL) and ongoing studies in Psychology and Neuroscience, I use stained glass to explore how our minds shape our experience.

I’m particularly fascinated by awe—an emotion now recognised and studied as a distinct emotion in psychology, often sparked when we encounter something vast, beautiful, or beyond comprehension. Stained glass is a natural catalyst for this: when light floods a space with colour, it transforms it, a natural reminder that something bigger than ourselves exists.

Recently, through glass, I've reimagine scientific ideas—like Penfield’s homunculus, a distorted map of the body in the brain—into visual metaphors. My aim is to spark curiosity, invite reflection, and show how art, science and wellbeing can illuminate each other.

About Me

I have a BA(Hons) in Fine Art Painting from UAL and am currently studying for a BSc in Psychology, driven by my fascination with neuroscience and the ways creativity and science are intwined.

I completed a Level 2 Stained Glass course at Morley College with Maria Zuletta, earning a Distinction, and have continued to develop my practice through glass painting, printing, and etching techniques. Recently I've aimed for my work to connect directly with psychology and neuroscience, combining concept with traditional craft.

One example is my stained-glass panel inspired by Penfield’s homunculus—a map of how the brain represents our body parts, where the hands, lips, and tongue appear huge compared to the rest of the body. This distortion reveals how our brains prioritise sensation and control, rather than physical appearance. By combining this model with Baroque-inspired colour and dramatic light, I aimed to depict the brain as both a place of perception and a vessel of awe.

Alongside my studio practice, I volunteer at the Natural History Museum, where I help children learn about science through crafts. This reflects my wider goal as an artist: to make complex ideas about the mind and the body not only understandable, but also exciting, playful, and awe-inspiring.

My work is guided by a simple aim: to use stained glass as a bridge between art and science, inspiring curiosity and offering moments of wonder.
